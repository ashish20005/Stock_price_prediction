Core Concepts

What makes time series data different?

Trend

Trend shows the long-term direction of a stock. If Reliance moves upward for several months, the model treats that as a persistent pattern rather than random noise.

Seasonality

Some price behavior repeats over fixed intervals, such as reactions around quarterly results, sector-wide cycles, or recurring market sentiment windows.

Stationarity

Many forecasting models work better when the statistical properties of the series stay stable over time. Differencing is often used to make data more stationary.

Lag

A lag compares today's price with previous days. Forecasting models use lagged values to learn how recent history influences the next movement.

Time Series Explanation

How stock forecasting data is organized and interpreted

Time series data is a sequence of observations recorded over time. In stock prediction, each row captures how a company's market behavior changes day by day, which allows a forecasting model to learn patterns from the past and estimate future prices.

What is time series data?

Time series data stores values in chronological order. Unlike regular tabular data, the order of records matters because yesterday's stock movement can influence today's direction. This makes time-based dependencies central to prediction.

Trend

Trend reflects the overall upward or downward direction of the stock over a longer period, helping models identify sustained growth or decline.

Seasonality

Seasonality captures repeating behavior over fixed intervals, such as reactions around quarterly earnings, market cycles, or recurring investor sentiment.

Noise

Noise is the irregular short-term fluctuation caused by unexpected events, rumors, or day-to-day market volatility that does not form a stable pattern.

Stationarity

Stationarity means the statistical behavior of the data remains fairly stable over time. Models like ARIMA often perform better when the series is transformed to be more stationary.

Techniques

Models used for forecasting stock prices

ARIMA

AutoRegressive Integrated Moving Average predicts future values using past observations, differencing, and error correction. It works well for strong linear patterns.

SARIMA

Seasonal ARIMA extends ARIMA by capturing repeating cycles. It is useful when stock behavior reflects periodic market or reporting rhythms.

LSTM

Long Short-Term Memory networks are a type of recurrent neural network that can learn longer dependencies and non-linear price dynamics. They help this project stand out.

Models Used

Why ARIMA and LSTM are common forecasting choices

ARIMA

ARIMA for structured historical patterns

ARIMA stands for AutoRegressive Integrated Moving Average. It uses past values, differencing, and forecast error relationships to estimate future data points. ARIMA works well when the stock series shows relatively stable linear behavior after preprocessing.

LSTM

LSTM for longer memory and non-linear patterns

LSTM is a deep learning model designed for sequential data. It can retain useful information from earlier time steps and is better suited for complex, non-linear market movement where traditional statistical models may miss turning points.

Model Evaluation

How forecast quality is measured

Model evaluation helps us understand how close the predicted stock prices are to actual values. Lower error values usually indicate a more reliable forecasting model.

MAE

Mean Absolute Error

MAE measures the average absolute difference between actual and predicted values. It is easy to interpret because it tells us, on average, how many price units the model is away from the true stock price.

12.4
RMSE

Root Mean Squared Error

RMSE gives more weight to larger prediction errors. This makes it useful when big misses matter more, such as during sudden stock movements or strong market volatility.

16.9
Accuracy

How to understand model accuracy

In stock forecasting, accuracy is not always expressed as a single percentage because prediction quality depends on how close the forecast is to actual prices over time. Instead, evaluation focuses on error-based metrics like MAE and RMSE, along with how well the model captures direction, turning points, and overall trend behavior.
